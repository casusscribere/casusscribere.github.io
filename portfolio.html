Kirk M. Lundblade is a scholar operating at the intersection of game studies, digital humanities, and public history. His research examines how digital historical games — particularly the Crusader Kings series — construct and contest histories through their mechanics, community discourse, and surrounding paratexts.

His doctoral dissertation, Crusading for Fun and Profit: An Examination of Ludohistorical Mode in the Crusader Kings Community, earned the UCF College of Arts and Humanities Outstanding Dissertation Award (2024). The project traces how platforms, communities of practice, and formal game affordances co-construct what he terms "ludohistorical mode" — the particular way historical games invite players to inhabit, revise, and produce history.

Lundblade's scholarship appears in the Journal of Gaming & Virtual Worlds, Popular Culture Studies Journal, and proceedings of Foundations of Digital Games (FDG) and DiGRA. His broader agenda encompasses procedural rhetoric, the Games-as-a-Service paradigm, online discourse analysis, and the methodological challenges facing qualitative researchers working at scale in digital community spaces.

Alongside his academic career, he works as a professional developer with deep expertise in web application development, test engineering, and data visualization — and as a technical educator at the graduate and undergraduate levels.

Great Works

2026 Conference
Bending the Arc of (Ludo)History: Discursive Contestations Within the (Hi)Story-Design-Space of Crusader Kings III
Foundations of Digital Games 2026 — Copenhagen, Denmark
Examines how scholarly grammars of historical possibility in play can be expanded to match discursive contexts where the possibility space is itself in flux within Games-as-a-Service community spaces. Offers a framework with implications for both historical game studies and game community research.
2025 Book Chapter
What Video Games Have Taught Us: Two Decades of Gaming and Learning
Historiographies of Game Studies: What It Has Been, What It Could Be
Uses Gee's landmark 2003 monograph as a focal lens for analyzing how games-and-learning scholarship served as a site of disciplinary boundary formation during game studies' early years, and how that early history continues to shape the field.
2024 Conference
Gods, Kings, and Historians: History and the Dual Diegesis of Crusader Kings in the Assemblage of Play
DiGRA 2024 — Guadalajara, MX
Analyzes the diegetic complexity of Crusader Kings III, exploring how overlapping diegetic perspectives — each rooted in distinct ideological framings — create a playground for historically-aware engagement, revision, and refutation of medieval historicity.
2023 Conference
If Your Empire Last More Than Two Centuries, You May Need to Speak to Your Doctor
Foundations of Digital Games 2023 — Lisbon, Portugal
Analyzes how community notions of historicity, historical accuracy, and culturally-situated rhetorical authority structure critiques of Crusader Kings III in online discourse under the GaaS paradigm.
2023 Conference
Sorting Things Out: Critically Assessing Reddit's Post Sorting Algorithms on Qualitative Analysis Methods
ACM SIGDOC 2023 — Orlando, FL
Examines how Reddit's post-sorting algorithms systematically shape the data available to qualitative researchers in online community spaces, with methodological implications for discourse analysis at scale.
2021 Journal
Watch Me Make History: Reenacting and Remaking the Past in Historical Game Live Streams
Popular Culture Studies Journal, vol. 9, pp. 69–87
Situates historical game live streaming within wider assemblages and contextual sites, examining how historical practice intersects with platform norms and community conventions in Twitch and YouTube streams.
2021 Conference
Crowning Achievements: A Historioludic Analysis of the Achievement Metagame in Historical Simulation Games
Foundations of Digital Games 2021 — Montréal, QC
Builds a typology of historical engagement through game achievements, examining how extra-ludic systems guide historical inquiry and the extent to which developers invest in shaping historical awareness through play.
2020 Conference
Civilizing Civilization (and beyond): A History of Historical Game Studies
Foundations of Digital Games 2020 — Bugibba, Malta
Traces the formation of historical game studies as a distinct field — its metalanguage, critical discourses, and taxonomic structures — from its origins in Civilization scholarship through its development as an interdisciplinary scholarly community.
2019 Journal
How the West (Was) Won: Unit Operations and Emergent Procedural Rhetorics of Colonialism in Europa Universalis IV
Journal of Gaming & Virtual Worlds, 11(3): 251–270
Uses procedural rhetoric and unit analysis to examine how the algorithmic implementations of Europa Universalis IV advance arguments about colonialism, historical determinism, and technological advancement at both macro and micro levels.
